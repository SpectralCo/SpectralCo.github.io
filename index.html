SpectralCo

SpectralCo is an independent research company developing rigorous mathematical frameworks at the intersection of pure mathematics and theoretical neuroscience. Our founding programme, Categorical Spectralism, constructs a functorial bridge between observable morphic structure and the spectral properties of systems whose internal dynamics cannot be directly accessed.

About

Who we are

SpectralCo is a research company committed to the highest standards of mathematical rigour. We work on foundational problems that resist established methods — problems where the standard assumption of accessible internal structure fails by construction — and develop the new mathematical machinery required to address them.

Our approach is resolutely interdisciplinary. The deepest open problems in consciousness science, theoretical neuroscience, and complex systems are fundamentally mathematical problems in disguise. We treat them as such.

Aims

SpectralCo is committed to open, rapid dissemination of research. We aim to produce one pre-reviewed preprint per week — releasing work at the frontier of the programme as it develops, rather than waiting for the full apparatus of formal publication. Speed and transparency are features, not concessions. The mathematical community is invited to engage with, critique, and build on each release as it appears.

Research Programme

Categorical Spectralism

The classification of systems with inaccessible internal structure is a foundational problem in mathematics and the sciences. At every major theoretical advance — from Newtonian mechanics to quantum field theory — categorical labels that appeared definitive were revealed to be approximations of a richer underlying structure. Classification is always provisional precisely because the objects being classified have inaccessible internal structure.

The appropriate mathematical response is not to replace those labels but to supplement them with a richer object that carries strictly more information.

Categorical Spectralism

CS Framework  ·  Founding Research Programme

Categorical Spectralism (CS) constructs a functor F from a category U of systems with inaccessible internal structure — with observable state transitions as morphisms — to the category Hilb of Hilbert spaces. The spectral properties of the resulting operator encode geometric and dynamical information about the system without requiring knowledge of the algebra governing its internal dynamics. The algebra, where it exists, is recovered as a consequence rather than stipulated as a premise.

From this construction CS derives the Spectral Complexity Index (SCI): a continuous, substrate-independent complexity measure computable in polynomial time from spectral data via standard eigendecomposition algorithms. The central conjecture of the programme — the Substrate-Independence Conjecture — holds that phenomenal conscious experience is characterised by categorical-spectral invariants that are preserved under the CS functor independently of the physical substrate in which they are realised.

CS is a strict generalisation of Koopman operator theory. When applied to a measure-preserving dynamical system with known state space, the CS functor reduces exactly to the Koopman operator. The CS construction proceeds from observable morphisms alone — precisely the regime Koopman theory cannot address. The relationship between CS and the Free Energy Principle is complementary: FEP describes the trajectory a system takes through its state space; CS characterises the spectral properties of that space.

Open Problems

I.

Whether the CS functor construction is a generalisation of spectral triples in a precise categorical sense, or an independent construction that subsumes them.

II.

Whether SCI subsumes the integrated information measure Φ as a special case — that is, whether SCI = f(Φ) for some function f when full internal enumeration is available.

III.

Whether the CS construction can be lifted to an ∞-categorical setting, connecting CS directly to Lurie's spectral algebraic geometry and providing the formal machinery needed to prove the central conjecture.

IV.

Whether systems minimising free energy under FEP converge to attractors whose spectral signatures are invariant under the CS functor — that free energy minimisation preserves SCI at equilibrium.

Current Work

Open Mathematical Programme

The CS framework identifies a structured set of open problems that must be resolved in sequence before the central conjecture can be proved. These problems are not independent curiosities — they form a dependency chain, each a necessary step toward the proof of Conjecture 6.4. They are stated here precisely so that collaborators can engage with individual components independently.

Foundational Problems

Prerequisites for all downstream work
3.9

Functoriality in general. Construct a faithful functor F: U → Hilb for an arbitrary system with inaccessible internal structure, and prove F is faithful — that F(f) = F(g) implies f = g. This is the primary foundational problem. Without it, the conjecture cannot be stated with the precision required for a proof.

3.11

Self-adjointness. Determine the conditions under which the operator F(f) derived from morphic structure is self-adjoint, and extend the framework to non-self-adjoint operators via pseudospectral methods. This is the most significant scope limitation of the current formulation.

Structural Problems

Refinements that strengthen the conjecture
3.22

Monotonicity of SCI. Prove that SCI is monotone with respect to a natural partial order on spectral distributions — that richer spectral distributions yield higher SCI values. A formal proof requires a precise definition of the partial order and a monotonicity argument for the weighted sum.

3.23

Boundedness of SCI. Establish that SCI(T) ≤ ‖T‖ · max Q_n for all positive self-adjoint T ∈ B(H). An upper bound in terms of the absolute boundary ‖T‖ would ensure SCI is always finite for bounded operators and provide a natural normalisation.

5.13

Directed connectome extension. Extend CS to directed connectome models using non-self-adjoint spectral theory or pseudospectral methods. This would allow SCI to be computed from effective connectivity rather than structural connectivity, significantly expanding the empirical scope of the consciousness application.

Conjecture-Level Problems

The proof programme for Conjecture 6.4
6.7

Characterisation of CS operators. Characterise the class C_CS ⊂ B(H) of operators that arise as functor images F(f) for some morphism f in some category U of undefined objects. Without knowing precisely what class of operators CS produces, the conjecture cannot be stated with full precision. This is the foundational characterisation problem.

6.10

Injectivity of SCI on CS spectral measures. Show that SCI is injective on spectral measures within C_CS — that SCI(T) = SCI(T′) implies the spectral measures of T and T′ are identical. This is the critical lemma and the most technically demanding component of the proof. It requires showing that the amplitude-weighted mean quality factor functional uniquely determines the full spectral distribution within the class of CS operators.

Empirical Problems

Validation of the framework against data
5.10

SCI ordering matches PCI ordering. Compute SCI from published connectome harmonic data and test whether SCIcon ordering matches the PCI ordering of Casali et al. [2013] — anaesthesia < wakefulness < REM sleep. This is the primary proposed empirical test of the consciousness application, falsifiable from existing published data.

5.14

SCI–Phi relationship. Determine whether SCI = f(Φ) for some function f when the system is fully enumerable. If true, IIT is a special case of CS for accessible systems — a significant theoretical unification. If false, the two frameworks make genuinely different predictions and their empirical disagreement would be scientifically informative.

5.15

Threshold conjecture. Determine whether there exists a critical value SCI* such that SCIcon(O) ≥ SCI* is necessary and sufficient for state O to be a conscious state in the phenomenal sense. This is the spectral formulation of the hard problem: a mathematical threshold replacing a categorical definition.

Connections to the Broader Mathematical Landscape

Future directions
I

Operator systems and operator spaces. The characterisation of C_CS (Lemma 6.7) is likely connected to the theory of operator systems and operator spaces, which provides tools for characterising classes of operators defined by structural conditions. The morphic structure of U may impose constraints on F(f) expressible as operator system conditions.

II

The moment problem. The injectivity of SCI on spectral measures (Lemma 6.10) is related to the classical moment problem in functional analysis: given a sequence of moments of a measure, when is the measure uniquely determined? SCI is a weighted first moment of the spectral distribution. Whether the full distribution is recoverable from SCI alone, or whether SCI must be augmented with higher-order functionals, is a question in the theory of moment problems.

III

Extension to ∞-categorical settings. Lifting CS to an ∞-categorical setting would connect the framework to Lurie's spectral algebraic geometry and potentially provide the higher categorical machinery needed to prove the conjecture. In such a setting, morphisms between state transitions, and higher morphisms between those, are systematically tracked — which may provide the right framework for Lemma 6.7.

Preprint · Draft
Categorical Spectralism: A Functorial Framework for Spectral Complexity and a Conjecture on Substrate-Independent Categorical Equivalence
Charles O  ·  SpectralCo

We introduce Categorical Spectralism (CS), a functorial framework for the analysis of systems with inaccessible internal structure. Beginning with a category U of undefined objects — systems whose internal algebra cannot be directly accessed — we construct a functor F: U → Hilb to the category of Hilbert spaces, recovering spectral properties from observable state transitions alone. From this construction we derive the Spectral Complexity Index (SCI), a continuous substrate-independent complexity measure computable in polynomial time. We state the Substrate-Independence Conjecture: that phenomenal conscious experience is characterised by categorical-spectral invariants preserved under F independently of physical substrate. We demonstrate that CS is a strict generalisation of Koopman operator theory, and identify its relationship to noncommutative geometry, integrated information theory, the Free Energy Principle, and higher category theory.

Publishing Philosophy

On peer review & journals

SpectralCo holds a considered and non-standard position on peer review, academic publishing, and what constitutes evidence of rigour in mathematical and scientific work. A full position paper is in preparation. The core argument is outlined below.

Position paper forthcoming

In preparation  ·  SpectralCo

The conventional peer review process — in which the validity and significance of a piece of work is determined by a small number of anonymous reviewers prior to publication — is not, we argue, the appropriate mechanism for establishing rigour in mathematical and theoretical research. The bottleneck it introduces is disproportionate to the epistemic work it performs.

Rigour in mathematics is established not by the judgement of individual reviewers but by the response of the community to the work itself: whether results are cited and built upon, whether proofs withstand scrutiny when exposed to the full field, whether the framework generates predictions that are tested and survive, whether open problems stated explicitly attract serious engagement. These are more demanding standards than a referee report, not less demanding ones.

SpectralCo will release all outputs as preprints immediately upon completion and submit selectively for formal publication where venue is strategically appropriate. We do not treat journal acceptance as a prerequisite for taking our own work seriously, nor as the primary signal by which others should evaluate it.

A full treatment of this position — including a survey of the empirical literature on peer review reliability, a formal argument about the epistemic structure of mathematical community validation, and a proposed alternative quality standard — is in preparation and will be released as a SpectralCo position paper.
